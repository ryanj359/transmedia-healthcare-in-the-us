Processed Foods

Diane Azrak

It's no secret America has a food problem. Between an obesity number of over 100 million Americans to the constant trends of homesteaders making their own foods from scratch, our ingredients just aren't cutting it. I can't count the number of social media posts I've seen of people who live their lives in America gluten-free and they're able to consume pasta in Europe without an issue. Or how about the amount of unpronounceable ingredients on the back of snack bags? Do you ever wonder what they all mean?

Let's start with a few definitions. A processed food is any food that has been changed from its original state. Say you saute an onion, that's processing it. Most of the foods we consume are processed in some way. Just because it's been processed, doesn't mean it's unhealthy (a common misconception). The issue is what comes with ultra processed foods, “which often include ingredients you would not usually have at home, such as preservatives, sweeteners and emulsifiers.” It's common in items that contain sweeteners or are packaged for longevity. Ultra processed foods actually make up about 60% of the American diet because they're ready to eat, but unfortunately they tend to be low in nutrients.

What is added to ultra processed foods? @hot.n.health Instagram blogger, Susan Ezon, breaks it down:

  • High Fructose Corn Syrup- “It disrupts the body's ability to regulate blood sugar and appetite. Since fructose is primarily processed in the liver, excessive intake can lead to fatty liver disease, and a higher risk of heart disease. HFCS is linked to increased cravings and overconsumption due to its effects on brain reward pathways. While small amounts aren#39t necessarily harmful, excessive consumption can lead to serious long-term health issues like obesity, insulin resistance, and type 2 diabetes.”

  • Allulose- “It's a sugar alcohol which is partially unable to be absorbed by the Gastrointestinal system so it can cause tons of gas and bloating.”

  • Gums and Stabilizers- Gums are added to food as thickening agents. “They're high in fiber and can cause bloating, gas, and diarrhea, especially in large amounts. Some people with sensitive stomachs (such as those with IBS) may experience worse symptoms.”

  • Hydrogenated vegetable oil- “They're trans fat which will raise your bad cholesterol, LDH, and lower your good cholesterol, HDL.”

  • Artificial colors- “They're linked to cancer, immunity issues, and hyperactivity in children. Let's also note that artificial colors in Europe have been outlawed for centuries!”

The discussion of healthcare of other countries is a tough comparison with a lot of nuance, however Ezon briefly shares “one of the most alarming things I've learned is how deeply embedded ultra-processed foods are in the American diet—engineered for addiction, stripped of nutrients, and linked to skyrocketing rates of obesity, diabetes, and heart disease.” Europe, on the other hand, has outlawed certain ingredients that America seems to have no problem adding to our diets such as the gum, Carrageenan, for example. “Numerous studies have shown an increase in gut inflammation, glucose intolerance, impaired insulin action and systemic inflammation with daily intake of carrageenan. Regardless of the studies, the FDA still approves this as an additive.”

The thing is, these ingredients have become almost financially impossible to avoid.

Ultra Processed foods are convenient, quick and cheap. Thrive Market, a healthy sustainable food brand, explains the costs factor. A food with more ingredients costs more than a food with less. Almost all the foods you see at a grocery store are manufactured by the same ten major food companies. As time went on they found their own ways to cut costs on manufacturing their products. “As the quantity of product increases, the cost of production decreases.” Most of these additives are quite low in cost because they tend to be made of corn, which has 90 million acres grown per year by the US.

“And while not everyone can afford the luxury of a non toxic cracker there are better options that are in the middle,” Ezon advised. “To do this I would advise consumers to get a little well versed in the world of food ingredients. Like reading this article! Learn a thing or two and bring it with you the next time you go shopping.”

Healthcare Costs & Climate Change in Southern California

Jill Ryan

There's no denying that the climate is changing; summers are getting warmer, winters are getting colder, oceans are rising, and so much more. One of the many states that climate change is heavily affecting is California. “Average summer temperatures in California have risen by approximately 3 degrees F (1.8℃) since 1896, with more than half of that increase occurring since the early 1970s.”

Simultaneously, the price of healthcare coverage has been on the rise. According to MEPS, the Medical Expenditure Panel Survey, “Average monthly premiums for families with employer-provided health coverage in California's private sector nearly doubled over the last 15 years, from just over $1,000 in 2008 to almost $2,000 in 2023.”

Could these two factors go hand-in-hand? I talked to Mike Ryan, a former California resident of 68 years, about his thoughts on this possibility.

  1. Can you tell me a bit about yourself?



  2. How long and what part of California did you live in?



  3. In those 68 years, how would you describe the changes in climate that you've observed in Southern California?



  4. Did you notice any shifts in seasonal patterns, like hotter summers or maybe even colder winters? Or less predictable weather in general?



  5. Aside from the predictability, did you notice over the years that things like wildfires, droughts, earthquakes or even extreme heat, became more common or severe?



  6. How did your health insurance premiums change over the years?



  7. Before you had the business plan, did you have personal coverage?



  8. Although you mentioned that you didn't notice a huge change, was there any time at all where you noticed your premium went up and it felt kind of unexpected, or anytime you saw a noticeable change?



  9. Do you think there were any other factors outside of Obamacare being introduced that would have caused that that spike in any way? Do you think that any of it could have been related to environmental risks in California at all?



  10. Were you ever personally affected by any natural disasters or environmental risks like maybe like wildfires or earthquakes?



  11. During those times where you were affected, did you have to use healthcare services more than than normal?



  12. Did your healthcare insurance provider ever reach out about coverage changes that were related to climate risks or emergency preparedness?



  13. So there was never any coverage added to help potentially prepare for a natural disaster in terms of health care?



  14. Do you wish it was? Would you have found that beneficial?



  15. Have you or anyone you know, experienced more health issues that you think could be related to environmental changes or natural disasters? Like asthma or, as you just said, heat exhaustion or allergies?



  16. Over the years that you lived in San Diego, were there times of year where it was harder to go outside because of heat or air quality? Did you find that happening more frequently over the years?



  17. Do you feel like health insurance companies and or the local and state government are doing enough to help people deal with the health impacts of not only climate change, but also natural disasters in California?



  18. Have you ever had or wanted to change your health plan or provider because of cost increases?



  19. Do you think these cost changes within the company were ever due to environmental risks or a recent natural disaster that had happened?



  20. Do you think that insurance companies in general, in regards to natural disasters and environmental risks, for lack of better words, put more of their time and effort into protecting property rather than the health of people?



  21. Do you think health insurance companies should be putting more thought into and care their coverage, as property insurance companies do in terms of like natural disasters?



  22. Do you think healthcare coverage and climate are connected?



  23. This might seem blunt, do you believe in climate change?

The Affordability of Drugs and Pharmaceuticals

Ash Steinson

Since 2000, the United States has seen a huge increase in healthcare spending,the rise in spending has shot up so much that by 2019 it doubled to 8.4 Trillion—before COVID-19 evan began. Professor Yaseen Hayajneh of Western Connecticut State University notes an important depiction of the current healthcare model in America: “the U.S. healthcare model is built around curing illness rather than preventing it.” This approach not only increases the cost of production but also slows the efficiency of helping people in need.

In the 1960s healthcare made up only about 5% of the nation's GDP spendings. In 2023, healthcare made up 17.6% of America's GDP. Inflation is also an important factor in the conversation about healthcare pricing. Prices for prescription and non-prescription drugs have risen. America has been spending more money and has been getting less in return for its value.

This high-cost, low-value system creates a number of issues. According to data, more than 51% of American adults who don't have insurance have delayed or will entirely skip treatments because they simply couldn't afford financing them. This statistic greatly jumps to 63% among lower income households earning less than $40,000 a year. In some cases patients may find themselves not even refilling life saving medication due to the cost.

About 41% of non-insured American adults are currently struggling to pay off their medical bills or are in debt. Some of the main causes of this crisis is the lack of regulation America has with drug pricing compared to other countries.There is significantly less regulation from the government when it comes to determining the prices of drugs for retail.

In Comparison to Canada we see a shift in some of the major points in the previous paragraphs. In 2024, Canada spent about 9.9% of its GDP on healthcare which is just under half of what the U.S. spends. Most essential medical services in Canada are fully covered by public funding, with minimal out-of-pocket costs for patients. The benefit of that results in less citizens having to be in debt for medical related reasons. The average Canadian contributes roughly $6,325 per year (USD) in taxes toward healthcare, while the average American spends just under $15,000. Additionally, Canada's healthcare system operates on a principle of equality—ensuring citizens or visitors from all economic status can receive the proper health care and attention equally. Whether a patient is wealthy or is living in poverty the government ensures to put the people first.

However recently there have been more efforts to help with drug pricing in the U.S. that are beginning to emerge. One example is Cost Plus Drugs, a company that was founded by Mark Cuban to help traditional pharmaceutical pricing by working directly with drug manufacturers. According to their website, prescription drug prices in the U.S. are about 2.5 times higher than in most other countries, largely due to a lack of pricing regulation and transparency. Cost Plus Drugs negotiates bulk pricing for generic medications, then applies only a 15% markup to cover operational costs—drastically lowering the price for consumers. Their goal is to make sure that people can get access to affordable and safe drugs are a rate that is more generous.

The current state of the U.S definitely relies on a profit based system but its ensuring to know some people are working to make a change.
